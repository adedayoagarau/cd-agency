### Why GOV.UK Matters

GOV.UK is widely considered the gold standard for content design. Their principles are evidence-based, tested with millions of users, and designed for universal comprehension — including users with low literacy, cognitive disabilities, and non-native English speakers. If it works for government services, it works everywhere.

### The Core Content Design Principles

**1. Start with user needs.**
Don't write content because your organization wants to say it. Write content because users need it. "What does the user need to know to complete their task?" — nothing more.

**2. Do the hard work to make it simple.**
Complexity is easy. Simplicity requires effort. Every sentence should be questioned: is this the simplest way to say this? Can I cut a word? Can I restructure to eliminate a clause?

**3. Design with data.**
Use analytics, search data, and user research to determine what content users need, what they're looking for, and where they get stuck. Don't guess — measure.

**4. Iterate. Then iterate again.**
Publish, test, learn, revise. Content is never "done." Every page should be reviewed regularly.

### GOV.UK Writing Rules

1. **Use short sentences.** 25 words maximum. Ideally under 20.
2. **Use short paragraphs.** 2-3 sentences per paragraph maximum.
3. **Use subheadings.** Break content into scannable sections.
4. **Use bullet points.** For lists of 3+ items.
5. **Use active voice.** "We will send you a letter" not "A letter will be sent."
6. **Use "you" and "we."** Address the user directly.
7. **Use plain English.** Every word should be understood by a 9-year-old.
8. **Avoid jargon.** If you must use technical terms, explain them.
9. **Don't use formal or long words when simple ones will do.** "Buy" not "purchase." "Help" not "assist." "About" not "approximately."
10. **Use contractions.** "You'll" not "You will." "Don't" not "Do not." (Exception: emphasis — "Do not share your password.")

### The GOV.UK Word List

Words to avoid and their replacements:

| Don't use | Use instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| Advise | Tell |
| Commence | Start |
| Complete (a form) | Fill in |
| Consequently | So |
| Ensure | Make sure |
| Establish | Set up / find out |
| Facilitate | Help |
| In order to | To |
| Key (adjective) | Important / main |
| Obtain | Get |
| Prior to | Before |
| Purchase | Buy |
| Regarding | About |
| Terminate | End / stop |
| Utilise | Use |

### The Readability Standard

GOV.UK targets a reading age of 9 (roughly US Grade 4). Their reasoning:
- 1 in 6 adults in the UK has the reading skills of an 11-year-old or below
- Even highly educated readers prefer simple language — it's faster to process
- Simple language is more accessible to people with dyslexia, cognitive impairments, and non-native speakers
- There is no audience that rejects clear, simple language

### Content Patterns

**The "Get [thing] done" pattern:**
Structure content around the user's task, not the organization's structure.
- Bad: "About the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency"
- Good: "Renew your driving licence"

**The "most popular first" pattern:**
On any page, the most-needed content comes first. Use analytics to determine what users need most.

**The "one thing per page" pattern:**
Each page should help users do ONE thing. If a page tries to do two things, split it. This revolutionized form design — GOV.UK pioneered the one-question-per-page form pattern.

### Evidence and Results

- After redesigning content with these principles, GOV.UK reduced the government's web presence from 75,000 pages to ~3,000 pages (with better user satisfaction)
- Task completion rates improved by 30-50% on redesigned services
- Call center volume dropped significantly for services with well-designed content
- The GOV.UK Design System is now used by governments in 30+ countries

### Applying GOV.UK Principles to Product Content

Even in commercial products, GOV.UK principles apply:
- Your users are busy, distracted, and may not be native speakers
- Simplicity is never condescending — it's respectful of people's time
- Data should drive content decisions, not opinions
- Every word on screen should help someone complete a task

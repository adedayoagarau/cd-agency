### About the Book

Steve Krug's *Don't Make Me Think* is the most widely read usability book ever written. Its central thesis is the title itself: the best interfaces require zero thought. Every moment a user spends figuring out the interface is a moment they're not spending on their actual task.

### Core Thesis

"If something requires a large investment of time — or looks like it will — it's less likely to be used." The job of design (and content design) is to make everything self-evident. If it can't be self-evident, make it self-explanatory. If it can't be self-explanatory, add brief explanation.

### Key Principles

**1. Don't make users think.**
- Navigation should be obvious: "Where am I?" "Where can I go?" "How do I get back?"
- Labels should be clear: if users have to interpret what a button means, the label has failed
- Links should set expectations: the user should know what they'll find before clicking

**2. Users don't read — they scan.**
- We don't read pages. We scan them, looking for words or phrases that catch our eye.
- Implication: make the right words catchable (bold, headers, links)
- Implication: structure content for scanning (lists, short paragraphs, meaningful headers)

**3. Users don't make optimal choices — they satisfice.**
- Users don't pick the best option. They pick the first reasonable option.
- Implication: the right choice needs to be the obvious choice
- Implication: don't give 10 options when 3 will do

**4. Users don't figure out how things work — they muddle through.**
- Users don't read instructions. They try things and see what happens.
- Implication: make actions reversible (undo)
- Implication: make errors recoverable (good error messages)
- Implication: make exploration safe

### Krug's Laws of Usability

**Krug's First Law:** Don't make me think.
**Krug's Second Law:** It doesn't matter how many times I have to click, as long as each click is a mindless, unambiguous choice.
**Krug's Third Law:** Get rid of half the words on each page, then get rid of half of what's left.

### Content Design Implications

**On navigation text:**
- "Utilities" is a bad label. "Help & Settings" is better.
- Navigation items should be immediately understood without context
- Use conventional labels: "Home," "About," "Contact," "Search"

**On page titles:**
- Every page needs a clear, descriptive title
- The title should match the link that brought the user here
- Titles frame everything that follows

**On instructions:**
- Nobody reads instructions. Make the interface work without them.
- If instructions are necessary, make them:
  - Brief (the absolute minimum)
  - Immediately visible (not hidden)
  - Specific (not general advice)

**On the "happy talk" problem:**
- "Happy talk" = content that takes up space without conveying information
- "Welcome to our amazing platform! We're so thrilled you're here!"
- Cut all happy talk. Users skip it anyway, and it pushes useful content below the fold.

### Actionable Takeaways

- **The trunk test:** On any page, users should be able to answer: Where am I? What are my options? How did I get here? Where's the search?
- **Omit needless words.** Krug's third law is ruthless: cut half, then cut half again.
- **Conventions are your friend.** Shopping cart icon, hamburger menu, search magnifying glass — use what people already know.
- **Usability testing is mandatory.** One afternoon of testing with 3-5 users teaches more than months of debate.
- **"It depends" is always the right answer** — then test to find out what it depends on.

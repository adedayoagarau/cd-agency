### About the Book

*Nicely Said: Writing for the Web with Style and Purpose* is one of the foundational texts for web content professionals. Written by Nicole Fenton (former Apple content strategist) and Kate Kiefer Lee (former Mailchimp communications director), it bridges the gap between traditional writing advice and the specific demands of digital content.

### Key Principles

**1. Know your audience before you write a word.**
- Who are they? What do they know? What do they need?
- What emotional state are they likely in when they encounter this content?
- What have they just done, and what will they do next?

**2. Voice is your brand's personality in writing.**
- Voice is consistent across all content — it's WHO you are
- Define it with adjectives and boundaries ("friendly, not silly")
- Test it by removing branding — can you still recognize your company?

**3. Tone adapts to the situation.**
- Same voice, different tone for different contexts
- Celebratory when users succeed, empathetic when they fail
- Match the emotional register to the user's likely state

**4. Every word is a choice.**
- Default to simple, common words
- Cut adverbs and adjectives that don't add meaning
- Read it aloud — if you stumble, rewrite

**5. Structure serves the reader.**
- Lead with the most important information
- Use headings, bullets, and short paragraphs
- One idea per paragraph, one action per step

### The Writing Process (Fenton & Lee's Framework)

1. **Research:** Understand the user, the context, and the goal
2. **Draft:** Write freely, then cut ruthlessly
3. **Revise:** Simplify language, tighten structure, check tone
4. **Test:** Read aloud, get feedback, test with users

### Actionable Takeaways for Content Design

- **Write a "This, Not That" voice guide** — it's more useful than abstract adjectives
- **Map tone to user scenarios** — create a matrix of content types × user emotions
- **Create a word list** — words your brand always/never uses
- **Edit for scannability** — users read 20-28% of words on a page
- **Cut "We're excited to announce"** and every other corporate cliché
- **Never punish users** for mistakes — errors are always the system's fault in copy
- **Be honest** — if something will take time, say so; if there's a limitation, disclose it

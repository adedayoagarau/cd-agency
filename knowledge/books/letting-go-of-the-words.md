### About the Book

Janice Redish's *Letting Go of the Words* is a comprehensive guide to writing for the web. Written by a usability pioneer and plain language expert, it provides detailed, evidence-based guidance for writing web content that people actually read and use.

### Core Thesis

Writing for the web is a conversation with your users. They come with questions; your content should answer those questions as quickly and clearly as possible. "Letting go" means releasing your attachment to the words you've written and focusing on whether they serve the reader.

### Key Principles

**1. People come to websites with questions.**
Every page visit is a question:
- "How much does this cost?"
- "How do I cancel my subscription?"
- "What does this feature do?"
- "Is this product right for me?"

Content should answer these questions directly, not force users to dig for answers.

**2. Write for scanning, not reading.**
- Use meaningful headings as questions users are asking
- Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences
- Use lists for 3+ items
- Bold key terms (but don't overdo it)
- Put the answer first, then the explanation

**3. Content is a conversation.**
Think of content as the person answering questions at a help desk:
- Good: "Returns are free for 30 days. Just use the prepaid label in your package."
- Bad: "Our return policy allows for the return of merchandise within a 30-day period from the date of purchase, provided the items are in their original condition."

**4. Use personas to write better.**
When writing, imagine a specific person:
- What do they already know?
- What jargon will confuse them?
- Are they rushed or browsing?
- What device are they using?

### Redish's Writing Framework

**Step 1: Plan before writing**
- Who are your readers?
- What do they need to do?
- What questions do they bring?
- What's the one thing they must take away?

**Step 2: Organize for your readers**
- Group content by user task, not by org structure
- Put the most important information first
- Create clear pathways (don't dead-end users)

**Step 3: Write clearly**
- Use "you" and active voice
- Short sentences (20 words or less)
- Common words
- One idea per sentence

**Step 4: Design for scanning**
- Clear headings that work as standalone navigation
- Short sections with breathing room
- Meaningful link text
- Tables for comparative information

**Step 5: Test and revise**
- Watch real users try to use your content
- Where do they get stuck? What do they skip? What confuses them?
- Revise based on observation, not opinion

### Key Frameworks

**The "conversation heading" technique:**
Write headings as questions users would ask:
- "How much does it cost?" instead of "Pricing"
- "What if I need to cancel?" instead of "Cancellation policy"
- "How do I get started?" instead of "Getting started guide"

**The "key message" technique:**
For every page, identify ONE key message. If the user reads nothing else, they should get this message. Put it in the first sentence.

### Actionable Takeaways

- **Write headings as user questions.** This forces user-centered content.
- **Cut your content in half.** Then see if it's still understandable. If yes, you had twice as many words as needed.
- **Test with 3-5 users.** Usability testing doesn't need to be expensive or formal.
- **Don't bury the answer.** The information users need most should require the least effort to find.
- **Every page is a landing page.** Users arrive from search, links, and bookmarks — not just the homepage. Every page must orient the user.
- **Tables beat paragraphs** for comparing options, listing features, or showing specifications.

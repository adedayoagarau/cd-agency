### Core Principle

Users don't want your product — they want progress in their lives. Every piece of UI text should help users achieve the "job" they hired your product to do. Content design bridges the gap between what the product does and what the user is trying to accomplish.

### The JTBD Formula

**"When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."**

Examples:
- "When I'm **checking out online**, I want to **feel confident my payment is secure**, so I can **complete my purchase without worry**."
- "When I'm **setting up a new tool**, I want to **get started quickly**, so I can **see value before committing time**."
- "When I **encounter an error**, I want to **understand what went wrong**, so I can **fix it and continue my task**."

### Applying JTBD to Content Design

**CTAs should promise the job outcome, not describe the feature:**
| Feature-focused | Job-focused |
|----------------|-------------|
| "Upload file" | "Add your document" |
| "Create account" | "Get started free" |
| "Enable notifications" | "Stay in the loop" |
| "Run report" | "See your results" |
| "Configure settings" | "Make it yours" |

**Onboarding should map to jobs:**
- Don't: "Welcome! Here's a tour of our features."
- Do: "What would you like to do first?" → map each choice to a job

**Error messages should preserve the job:**
- Don't: "Upload failed. Error code 413."
- Do: "Your file is too large. Compress it or choose a smaller file to continue."
- The user's job is "add my document" — the error message should keep them on that path.

**Empty states should connect to jobs:**
- Don't: "No data available."
- Do: "No sales this week yet. When orders come in, you'll see your revenue here."

### The Four Forces of Progress

When users consider switching behaviors (or using a feature), four forces are at play:

1. **Push** — dissatisfaction with current situation ("This is too slow")
2. **Pull** — attraction to new solution ("That looks easier")
3. **Anxiety** — fear of the new ("What if I lose my data?")
4. **Inertia** — comfort with the status quo ("I know how to do it the old way")

Content design addresses all four:
- **Push/Pull:** Headlines and CTAs that articulate the better outcome
- **Anxiety:** Reassurance copy ("You can undo this," "We won't share your data")
- **Inertia:** Low-commitment language ("Try it free," "Takes 30 seconds")

### Functional vs. Emotional vs. Social Jobs

Every user action has three dimensions:
- **Functional:** "I need to send this invoice" → clear, task-oriented copy
- **Emotional:** "I want to feel professional" → polished, confident tone
- **Social:** "I want my team to see I'm organized" → status/sharing features

Good content design addresses at least the functional job explicitly and the emotional job through tone and word choice.

### Job Stories vs. User Stories

| User Story | Job Story |
|-----------|-----------|
| "As a user, I want to filter results" | "When I have too many results, I want to narrow them down so I can find what I need quickly" |
| "As an admin, I want to export data" | "When I need to report to stakeholders, I want to get data in a format they expect" |

Job stories lead to better content because they capture context and motivation, not just features.

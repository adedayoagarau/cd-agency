### Core Principle

Behavior happens when three elements converge at the same moment: **Motivation** (the user wants to), **Ability** (the user can), and a **Prompt** (something triggers the action). B = MAP. If any element is missing, the behavior won't happen.

### The B=MAP Formula Applied to Content

**Motivation** — Why should the user act?
- Content that communicates value, urgency, or relevance
- "Save 2 hours per week" (benefit)
- "Offer ends tonight" (scarcity)
- "Join 10,000 teams already using..." (social proof)

**Ability** — Can the user act easily?
- Content that reduces friction and perceived effort
- "Takes 30 seconds" (time)
- "No credit card required" (cost)
- "3 simple steps" (simplicity)
- Clear labels that don't make users think

**Prompt** — Is the user being asked to act?
- A clear, visible call to action at the right moment
- "Start your free trial" (direct prompt)
- "Your report is ready — view it now" (trigger)
- Notifications, tooltips, inline suggestions

### Motivation Drivers (Fogg's Core Motivators)

| Motivator | Increase | Decrease |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Pleasure/Pain** | "Enjoy a clutter-free inbox" | "Stop wasting time on manual tasks" |
| **Hope/Fear** | "Grow your audience" | "Don't miss important updates" |
| **Social acceptance/rejection** | "Trusted by teams at Google" | "Don't fall behind competitors" |

### Ability Factors (Simplicity Chain)

A task is only as easy as its hardest element. Six factors determine ability:

1. **Time** — "1-minute setup" reduces perceived time cost
2. **Money** — "Free," "No hidden fees" reduces financial friction
3. **Physical effort** — "One click," "Drag and drop" signals ease
4. **Mental effort** — Simple labels, familiar patterns, no jargon
5. **Social deviance** — "Everyone's doing it" normalizes the action
6. **Routine** — "Works with your existing workflow" reduces change

**Content design principle:** You can't always increase motivation, but you can almost always increase ability through clearer, simpler content.

### Cialdini's Principles in UI Copy

Robert Cialdini's six principles of influence, applied to microcopy:

1. **Reciprocity:** Give value before asking — "Here's your free report. Upgrade for weekly insights."
2. **Commitment:** Start small — "Just add your email" before "Complete your profile"
3. **Social proof:** "4,500 reviews, 4.8 stars" / "Sarah from your team invited you"
4. **Authority:** "Recommended by security experts" / "Built on AWS infrastructure"
5. **Liking:** Conversational, human tone / "We're here to help"
6. **Scarcity:** "3 spots left" / "Available until Friday" (use ethically — only when true)

### Ethical Application

The behavior model is powerful and must be applied responsibly:
- **Never manufacture false urgency** ("Only 2 left!" when inventory is unlimited)
- **Never use dark patterns** (confusing unsubscribe flows, hidden costs)
- **Always enable the reverse action** (if they can subscribe in one click, they can unsubscribe in one click)
- **Motivation should be honest** — describe real benefits, not manufactured anxiety
- **Prompts should respect attention** — don't interrupt flow for low-value actions

### Content Design Checklist (B=MAP)

For any CTA or action prompt, verify:
- [ ] Is the motivation clear? (Why should I do this?)
- [ ] Is the ability high? (Can I do this easily?)
- [ ] Is the prompt visible and well-timed? (Am I being asked at the right moment?)
- [ ] Is the language honest and ethical?

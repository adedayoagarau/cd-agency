### Domain Context

SaaS/B2B content serves multiple audiences simultaneously — end users, administrators, decision-makers, and technical implementers. Content must be functional for daily users while also conveying value to buyers and supporting complex workflows with diverse permission levels.

### Key Challenges

1. **Multiple personas, one product.** An admin, an end user, and a billing contact see different things and have different needs.
2. **Onboarding is critical.** Users who don't activate within the first session rarely return. Content drives activation.
3. **Collaboration means shared language.** When teams use a tool together, terminology must be intuitive for everyone.
4. **Feature density.** Enterprise tools are complex — content must tame that complexity without hiding capability.

### Content Patterns by Feature

**Onboarding:**
- Segment immediately: "What will you use [Product] for?" → personalize the experience
- Show value fast: guide users to their first meaningful action within 5 minutes
- Progress indicators: "3 of 5 steps complete. Next: Invite your team."
- Skip options: "Skip for now — you can do this later in Settings"
- Checklist pattern: concrete tasks, each completable in 1-2 minutes

**Dashboards:**
- Lead with what changed: "Revenue up 12% this week" not "Here is your dashboard"
- Contextual labels: "Monthly Active Users: 1,234 (+5% vs. last month)"
- Empty dashboard states: "Your analytics will appear here once your site gets traffic"
- Data recency: "Updated 5 minutes ago" — staleness must be visible

**Settings and admin:**
- Group by task, not by feature: "Manage your team" not "User administration"
- Explain consequences: "Removing a member revokes their access to all projects"
- Progressive disclosure: basic settings visible, advanced behind "Show advanced"
- Roles and permissions: "Admin — Can manage team members, billing, and all projects"

**Pricing and upgrade flows:**
- Compare plans with clear differentiators: feature matrices with checkmarks
- Anchor on the recommended plan: "Most popular" or "Best for teams"
- Be transparent about limits: "Free plan: 3 projects, 1 GB storage"
- Trial endings: "Your trial ends in 3 days. Upgrade to keep your data and features."
- No hidden costs: show annual/monthly breakdown upfront

**Collaboration features:**
- Clear ownership: "Created by Sarah · Last edited 2 hours ago"
- Activity feeds: "[Name] [action] [object] [time]" — "Alex commented on Q3 Report · 1 hour ago"
- Notifications: specific and actionable — "Maria assigned you a task: Update homepage copy"
- Sharing: "Anyone with the link can view. Only team members can edit."

### Role-Based Content

| Role | Content focus | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **End user** | Task completion, efficiency | "Drag tasks to reorder your priorities" |
| **Admin** | Control, security, management | "Team members added after today will require SSO" |
| **Decision-maker** | Value, ROI, comparison | "Teams using [Product] save an average of 5 hours per week" |
| **Developer** | Integration, API, customization | "Use our REST API to sync data with your existing tools" |

### Upgrade and Retention Copy

**Soft limits:**
- "You've used 8 of 10 projects on your free plan. Upgrade for unlimited projects."
- Not: "Limit reached. Upgrade now."

**Feature discovery:**
- "Did you know? You can automate this workflow with templates." (contextual, not intrusive)

**Churn prevention:**
- "Before you go — is there anything we can help with?"
- "Your team has 3 active projects. Downgrading will archive projects over the free limit."
- Show what they'll lose specifically, not abstractly

### Case Studies

**Notion:** Minimal, flexible content. "One workspace for everything" — their content avoids prescribing use cases, letting users define their own. Lesson: for flexible tools, content should enable, not constrain.

**Figma:** Collaboration-first content. "You" and "your team" everywhere. Lesson: when the product is collaborative, content should reflect shared ownership.

**Linear:** Developer-focused project management with opinionated, concise content. No marketing fluff in the product. Lesson: B2B products can have strong, distinctive voice.

**HubSpot:** CRM with heavy onboarding investment. Setup wizards, contextual help, and "getting started" checklists. Lesson: complex products need guided activation, not just documentation.

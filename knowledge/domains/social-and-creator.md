### Domain Context

Social and creator platforms face unique content design challenges: content moderation at scale, trust and safety communication, onboarding into communities (not just tools), and supporting both consumers and creators. The content must serve people in emotionally charged situations — sharing personal moments, facing harassment, building businesses.

### Key Challenges

1. **Trust and safety.** Content around reporting, blocking, and moderation must be clear and empathetic.
2. **Two-sided markets.** Creators and consumers have different needs, vocabulary, and motivations.
3. **Community norms.** Content must establish and enforce behavior standards without feeling authoritarian.
4. **Emotional intensity.** Social interactions are personal — errors, restrictions, and changes feel personal too.

### Content Patterns by Feature

**Profile and identity:**
- Setup: "Tell people about yourself" not "Complete your profile"
- Bio prompts: "What are you passionate about?" — open-ended, not corporate
- Display name vs. username: clearly differentiate — "Display name is what others see. Username is your unique @handle."
- Privacy: "Who can see your posts?" with clear options — "Everyone / Followers only / Only you"

**Content creation:**
- Clear constraints: "280 characters" / "Up to 10 images" / "Videos up to 15 minutes"
- Encouraging drafts: "Draft saved. Come back anytime to finish."
- Publishing: "Post" not "Submit" (social language, not form language)
- Engagement feedback: "12 likes · 3 comments · 1 share" — real-time, specific

**Content moderation:**
- Removal notification: "Your post was removed for violating our community guidelines on [specific policy]."
- Always explain which rule: vague "policy violation" erodes trust
- Appeal path: "If you think this was a mistake, you can appeal."
- Warning: "This content may contain sensitive material. [View] / [Go back]"
- Reporting: "Thanks for reporting. We'll review this and let you know the outcome."

**Community guidelines:**
- Write rules in plain language, not legalese
- Give examples: "Harassment includes repeated unwanted contact, threats, and targeted insults."
- Frame positively where possible: "Be respectful" before "Don't harass"
- Enforcement transparency: "If you violate these guidelines, your content may be removed and your account may be restricted."

### Trust and Safety Content

| Situation | Tone | Pattern |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Reporting abuse** | Supportive, serious | "We take this seriously. Your report is confidential." |
| **Account restricted** | Factual, clear, fair | "Your account is restricted for 24 hours due to [specific violation]. You can still read posts but not comment." |
| **Account suspended** | Formal, specific, appeal path | "Your account has been suspended for [reason]. Review our guidelines and appeal if you believe this is an error." |
| **Safety resource** | Gentle, non-judgmental | "If you or someone you know is in danger, here are resources that can help." |
| **Content warning** | Neutral, informative | "The author marked this as sensitive content." |

### Creator-Specific Content

- **Monetization:** Transparent eligibility: "You need 1,000 followers and 10,000 views to qualify for the partner program."
- **Analytics:** Actionable insights: "Your audience is most active on Tuesdays at 7 PM" not just data tables
- **Payouts:** Clear timing: "Earnings are paid monthly. Your next payout: $234.50 on April 1."
- **Policy changes:** Direct communication: "Starting March 1, we're changing how recommendations work. Here's what this means for your content."

### Case Studies

**Instagram:** Content warnings and sensitivity filters. "This post may contain sensitive content" with explicit user choice. Lesson: give users agency over what they see without hiding content entirely.

**Discord:** Community-building language. "Welcome to the server!" / "Set up your roles." Gaming-native vocabulary that feels natural to the audience. Lesson: match your content language to your community's language.

**YouTube:** Creator-focused content design for monetization, copyright, and analytics. "Your video matched third-party content" — clear, specific, with next steps. Lesson: complex policy situations need plain-language explanations with escape hatches.

**Substack:** Writer-first positioning. "Start writing" not "Create content." Simplicity in creation tools. Lesson: the creation experience should feel as lightweight as possible — complexity belongs in settings, not in the creative flow.
